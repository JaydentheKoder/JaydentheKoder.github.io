Keep it right between appointments

A fresh style is an investment — here's how to protect it in a dorm, through practices, and through Ohio weather, so it lasts until the next trip.

✂️ Cuts, fades & waves

Daily: brush morning and night (soft or medium brush for waves), and moisturize — a little leave-in or light oil keeps your scalp from flaking under a fresh fade.

At night: durag or wave cap, every night. It keeps your lineup crisp longer, trains waves, and protects against cotton pillowcase friction. A satin pillowcase is a good backup for nights you skip it.

Between cuts: a fade looks its best for about 2 weeks. If the next trip is 3+ weeks out, keep edges tidy and let the top grow — resist letting a friend with clippers "just clean it up" unless you trust them with your hairline.

🪢 Braids & twists (weeks 1–8)

Week 1: if it's too tight — tension bumps, headache, edges pulling — that's not something to push through. A warm shower on your scalp helps loosen; if it doesn't ease in a couple days, tell the office so the braider can adjust. Tight braids can cause real, lasting edge damage.

Every night: satin bonnet or scarf. This is the single biggest thing that decides whether braids look good in week 5.

Weekly: oil your scalp (lightweight oil on the parts), and refresh frizz with mousse smoothed over the braids. Cleanse your scalp every 1–2 weeks — diluted shampoo or scalp cleanser dabbed on the parts, rinsed gently, then dry your roots fully so they don't stay damp.

Takedown: 6–8 weeks is the max. Past that, new growth tangles and matting starts — schedule the takedown/next style trip in week 6, not week 9. Budget a couple hours to detangle after takedown before any new style.

🔒 Locs

Starter phase (first 2–3 months): hands out of your hair, no heavy washing — when you do wash, use a residue-free shampoo and a stocking cap to keep coils intact. Skip conditioner early; it loosens baby locs.

Ongoing: retwist every 4–6 weeks (that's what the trip board is for). Between retwists: oil your scalp, sleep in a bonnet or loc sock, and don't over-retwist — too-frequent twisting thins roots.

Athletes: sweat is fine, buildup isn't. Rinse or use a witch-hazel scalp refresher after heavy weeks, and let locs dry fully — damp locs held under a beanie can mildew.

🌀 Natural hair & wash day in a dorm

Wash day (weekly or biweekly): detangle with conditioner in and a wide-tooth comb, ends to roots, before shampooing. Deep condition when you can — even 15 minutes under a shower cap while you do homework counts.

Moisture routine: liquid, then cream, then oil (the LOC method) on damp hair. Dorm heaters are brutally dry in winter — up your moisture game from November to March.

Drying: microfiber towel or an old t-shirt, never rough terrycloth rubbing. A cheap hooded-dryer attachment or diffuser is worth it if you style wet.

Silk press holders: wrap it at night, dodge rain and steamy bathrooms — it reverts when wet, and that's normal, not ruined hair.

🏃🏾 Sports, swim & sweat

Practice season: sweat mostly hits your scalp — blot with a cloth, refresh with a light braid spray or witch-hazel mix, and wash your scalp more often than usual. Braids and cornrows are the most practice-proof styles; the style finder can point you there.

Swimming: soak your hair with clean water before the pool (hair full of clean water absorbs less chlorine), wear a swim cap over it, and rinse immediately after. A leave-in conditioner under the cap adds a barrier.

Helmets: a thin durag or wave cap under a helmet saves your lineup and cuts friction on braids.
